## Week 2
### What are some of the constraints and tensions that Hayles notes have motivated writers to experiment in the field of E-Lit?
**In reference to passage from *Hayles, (2007) ‘E-Lit: what is it?’*:**
> Readers come to digital work with expectations formed by print, including extensive and deep tacit knowledge of letter forms, print conventions, and print literary modes. Of necessity, electronic literature must build on these expectations even as it modifies and transforms them. At the same time, because electronic literature is normally created and performed within a context of networked and programmable media, it is also informed by the powerhouses of contemporary culture, particularly computer games, films, animations, digital arts, graphic design, and electronic visual culture. In this sense electronic literature is a "hopeful monster" (as geneticists call adaptive mutations) composed of parts taken from diverse traditions that may not always fit neatly together. Electronic literature tests the boundaries of the literary and challenges us to re-think our assumptions of what literature can do and be.

The traditionalist approach to both print and digital literature has pushed writers into the expansive world of E-literature. A reader has engrained expectations and methods of engagement when consuming a work of literature that stifle its possibilities and ignore the cultural state of the world. Although E-literature has sprouted from traditional print literature, the genre is provoked by a variety of other cultural avenues, such as the ones mentioned above: computer games, internet culture, social media etc. The neglect of contemporary culture prominent in traditional literature and the tension this ignorance precipates has inspired writers to traverse deep into these unchartered waters. What Hayles (2007) outlines is a chaotic and sometimes confused mode of creation thats both exciting and confusing at the same time. It is this uncertainty and possibility that has propelled many into the field. 

### In terms of AI and ethics, consider where your own boundaries are in terms of what is unacceptable or acceptable use of AI. What do you see as potential ethical use-cases of AI (e.g., for research, for furthering open-source knowledge, for testing new areas of learning)? What are your hopes for the future of AI?
Although I am apprehensive of adopting AI into any form of creative practice, and approach it with a level of resentment, I feel as though it is doing things that are largely beneficial to human existence. I am cautious of falling into the routine human response of hate born from fear of the unknown. 

From a utopian perspective, AI is, or can be, taking away much of the mechanical and monotonous work from everyday life. In theory, this handing over could free us up to be truly creative — to get to know ourselves in ways that previously weren't possible. With the mechanical movements of the mind delegated to an LLM, we open new space for something more substantial and fulfilling to take place. 

On the other hand, one could argue that this process removes the container for creative action to take place. In the monotony, in the mundane processes of ordinary life. we find the will and need to create. The mechanical nature of certain thoughts and tasks allows creation and human endeavour to take place. If we outsource these things to LLM’s we might sacrifice the structure and craft that allows for artistic and philosophical creation to take place. 

Thus, I approach the topic of AI with great caution. My hope for AI is what public education promised the world when it was introduced in the 19th century. To provide free and open access to resources and education. To bring about a new age of information that wasn’t previously possible. If this helps humanity progress both in science, technology and art, then I can’t see an objection. What I am afraid of is this revolution’s byproducts: convenience born laziness and the centralisation of data and information. I don’t want AI to be a technology of secrets and algorithmic surveillance. A centralised entity shouldn’t decide what’s right or real or valuable. 

I am afraid of not understanding but just accepting it as a new overlord. Through familiarising myself with AI, I hope to find some sort of control and understanding.
